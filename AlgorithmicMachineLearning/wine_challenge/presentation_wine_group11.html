Comments

The histograms above allow us to extract various trends and information about each feature for the redwine dataset.

First there are some features that seem to be more or less similar or independent of quality. These features will not allow us to give any specification to classify the wine's quality. Fixed acidity, residual sugar, density, or pH. Also, free and total sulfur dioxide are low for good and bad wines and high for middle quality wines.

On the other hand, there are some features that seem to have a linear influence on the quality and will allow us to classify the wines by quality easily.

  • Volatile acidity goes down with quality
  • Citric acid goes up with quality
  • Chlorides goes down with quality
  • Sulphates goes up with quality
  • Alcohol goes up with quality

Some features are more or less correlated with quality, but we cannot pretend to remove arbitrarily some features according to the fact that we will lose global information and it can mistrain the data. Thus we decide to keep all features.

Trying to apply a RFECV method (Recursive Feature Elimination) and also a PCA (Principal Component Analysis) for feature reduction we noticed that these methods are costly and do not improve our dataset much. Indeed, the datasets are taken from UCI Machine Learning Datasets that offer very clean datasets.

We choose then to keep all the features given.

1.4. Data preprocessing¶

Following the tip, we will divide our dataset into two different qualities, good and not good, using the threshold quality >= 7.

Also, it seems that many features are irrelevant when estimating a wine's quality. We will try to do some feature elimination and feature decomposition using RFE and PCA techniques respectively.

2. Model Selection¶

In this part, we'll apply different learning models, in order to compare results. Knowing that the class repartition are unbalanced (~80% good and ~20% bad), we will use two major metrics to go along with the accuracy because this simple metric can hide bad results. AUC and LogLoss will help us to estime the real efficiency of our results. The higher AUC is the better is the model. The lower is the logloss the better is the model.

Conclusion¶

After having worked on three different models, we concluded that globally all the models work fine for redwine but less for white wine. Probably we have not enough characterisitic features for white wine, such as the robe of the wine according to the fact that white wines hold much sugar than red ones.

Concerning the three models, we arrived rapidely with sufficient results using Logistic Regression with really low AUC, we then decided to push the work a little harder using a KNN Classifier. But it appears not to be so much different from the Logistic Regression even if it is more time consuming. Thus, we finally decided to work on a Random Forest Classifier and optimizing its parameters. We arrived to great results in training with high AUC value but bad ones in testing. The overfitting is probably due to the optimizing part which was too complex.

To sum up, the best model that can be used is actually the Logistic Regression.
